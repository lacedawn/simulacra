---
title: "The Transsexual Matrix"
date: "2026-05-01"
description: "Applying Butlerian gender theory to the aesthetic and interpretive apparatus of online trans communities"
---
There is something very interesting about trans women in online spaces, it is almost undeniable and ignoring its existence is like being ignorant to a very self evident phenomenon. That is the emergence of a large amount of stereotypes, ones that have nothing to do with the trans identity inherently, totally unrelated. And I think in this respect transgender stereotypes are different from cisgender stereotypes. I'm talking about the Blahaj, Fallout New Vegas, jungle music, software development, Ultrakill, etc.. Though of course there are some stereotypes that in a way relate to the identity itself like striped thigh high socks for example. in this article, I'm going to try to apply a Butlerian gender analysis to explain why these stereotypes are so consistent and why the possibility of them disappearing is a very small one. 
Butler argued in *Gender Trouble* (1990) and *Bodies That Matter* (1993) that there is no essential gender, no inner truth that the performance of norms expresses. "Gender" is produced through the "stylized repetition of acts through time", though these acts are not a choice made by a pre-existing subject but rather the "doer" is a fiction produced by the "deed". This process occurs as norms are performed repeatedly within a social system until the performance itself dissolves, appearing instead as a natural given. What's so interesting, and building on Butler, is that this mechanism seems to be operating beyond the traditional categories of men and women into a newer and stranger social framework, that is the online trans community, where entirely new norms, new citational repertoires, new regulatory fictions get produced. Production that is so acceleratingly rapid and visible that the usual invisible machinery of gender, for a brief moment, seems to be clear.

There is a distinction that needs to be made first. The distinction between self-explanatory stereotypes and strange ones. The former one such as striped thigh highs, cat ears, basic skirts, etc. These ones are intelligible as trans stereotypes in a very obvious way: they are objects and aesthetics that belong to a clear feminine register, and their presence can be explained, however reductively, as trans women expressing a feminine identity that relates to womanness. The latter, though, is stranger and more interesting. The Blahaj has no intrinsic relationship to womanhood, neither does Fallout: New Vegas. breakcore is also not a historically feminine genre, software engineering is also a male dominated field. These objects carry no prior feminine charge (in contrast to the stereotypes we discussed earlier), and yet they carry weight identical to typically feminine objects such as thigh highs. The identity-adjacent stereotypes function within what might be called a heteronomous citational chain: they derive their normative force from outside the community, from the social system that they exist in and the cultural codes that associate these objects with femininity. The community didn't invent the feminine valence of skirts, it inherited it from the outside. For example, when a trans girl is wearing thigh highs or a skirt she is citing a norm that precedes and exceeds the online community. It's a norm that was produced by a broader apparatus Butler describes in Gender Trouble: the mechanism by which "gender reality is created through sustained social performances" until those performances naturalize and stabilize themselves as a "coherent" identity, which is what remains. And the performances that predates that identity now seem like they come after the identity. It is seen as if the identity is simply preforming acts that are inherent to itself rather than acts that produced that identity in the first place. The community here is a conduit for an already existing citational chain, not the origin of a new one.

While the community-internal stereotypes are doing something different. They are not citing norms that relate to femininity itself, but rather, they are citing *themselves*, or they are producing entirely new norms that previously had no relation to any "stable" identity. And they pushed further by latching those norms onto themselves. Breakcore isn't a feminine genre, it is, in a way, a *trans girl* genre, undeniably so. And trans girl in not derived from predating norms, but rather a positive identity category that has accumulated its own citational repertoire and regulatory apparatus. Its own field of performances. Butler's analysis in *Bodies That Matter* is ultimately limited to the heterosexual matrix as the regulatory apparatus that gives the citational chain its force. The subject, she argues, is "constituted through the force of exclusion and abjection". Which basically means that the formation of the subject required identification with the normative phantasm, and this identification "takes place through a repudiation without which the subject cannot emerge" (Bodies That Matter, Introduction). Butler is essentially saying that for a coherent subject to form, it identifies with a certain norm (for her, that is the heterosexual matrix) and this identification can only happen through a certain rejection to something that *fails* the heterosexual matrix. What is being repudiated is the abject figure that haunts the intelligible subject as its constitutive outside. In the heterosexual matrix, that abject figure is, broadly, the queer. The body whose desire or presentation does indeed fail the heterosexual matrix. That abject figure is expelled to what Butler calls "unlivable" and "uninhabitable." So, what is the analogous structure in the online trans community? There is one and it is certainly not too hard to find. The community produces its intelligible subject, that is a trans woman through a corresponding set of repudiations including: the egg, that is the pre-transition self who is not yet aware of their trans identity and is still captive to a performed masculinity that is now readable as denial (here I am speaking of the egg position itself that the intelligible trans girl subject has moved beyond and defined herself against. The abjection here is retrospective rather than literal social abjection). The inverse figure: the trans woman marked by the community itself as failing be it aesthetically, politically or socially whose very behavior is seen as inauthentic, embarrassing or threatening the community's coherence. The detransitioner in specific framings. These are the abject figures to the transsexual matrix, the constitutive outside that through them, the intelligible subject of the transsexual matrix is formed. The finalized, recognizable ideal. The community's citational chain has stakes because getting it wrong equals real social costs within the space where these identities are formed and maintained, in the transsexual matrix.

After this was established, applying a Butlerian analysis can be done. These stereotypes are not just aesthetic or arbitrary associations, they function as intelligibility markers, a semiotic method for producing and recognizing the intelligible subject within the community's regulatory apparatus. The Blåhaj becomes a citation in a citational chain that signals membership in the subject position. The performance of owning a Blåhaj withers to be a mere act of purchasing a commodity and becomes a part of the identity, a signifier. The same object, if held by someone outside the community, means nothing. It says nothing about their identity. Meaning that its societal value is entirely positional and dependent on the citational context that the community has built, maintains and repeats. Producing these community-specific norms over and over again. Though Butler can only explain why the chain is sticky, the reason being is that it reproduces itself through repetition of such performances, Butler cannot explain why these particular objects were recruited rather than others. The sociologist Dick Hebdige in *Subculture: The Meaning of Style* (1979) offers an agreeable account for this phenomenon through describing what he calls subcultural bricolage. He tracks the same basic phenomenon: subcultures "cobble together styles out of the image and material cultures available to them," selecting objects that are seen as ordinary in a wide context, ones that can be re-signified, relocated within a new discursive context, though not all objects are equally available for re-signifying. Objects that are already slightly dissonant and already associated with outsider positions in the mainstream culture are more available for this recruitment than objects firmly embedded in dominant cultural codes. Of course, this explanation does not account for *all* community-internal stereotypes such as computer science but I think it neatly explains ones like Fallout: New Vegas for example. It was already a cult object, associated with certain sensibility. Ironic, melancholic, aesthetically eccentric, beloved by people who found the mainstream gaming landscape to be too frictionless. Breakcore is deliberately abrasive and technically extreme. It exists in an explicit opposition to commercial electronic music. These objects were already held by people who had a certain relationship with cultural outsiderness. The community recruited these things into a new citational chain, over time the recruitment grew into something bigger: the objects became associated with the identity itself and the identity became associated with the objects. Though I need to be honest that this has been an attempt at explaining some of the stereotypes related to the transsexual matrix, it is not total. There are other community-internal stereotypes that Hebdige's theory cannot explain like Linux or computer science. A superficial analysis of such phenomenons would link these two to the correlation between gender identity issues and ASD, but this isn't my subject here. 

But the transsexual matrix's regulatory function does not stop at aesthetics, the Blåhaj and breakcore are the visible surface of a much deeper process. That is the production of interpretive frameworks through which ambiguous inner experience gets organized into legible identity. The stakes here become genuinely serious. Consider, for example, the debate around autogynephilia. The Blanchardian typology, whatever its empirical deficiencies, was asking a certain question: what is the relationship between erotic experience and gender identity in trans women? The clinical apparatus was asking for an answer in a specific form: locate your truth in your erotic history, confess the origin and the root of your desire. It was asking subjects to submit their inner lives to taxonomic judgments. It is, in Foucault's terms in the History of Sexuality Vol. 1 (1976), a confessional apparatus as it produces the truth it claims to discover. While the transsexual matrix produces a counter-apparatus. Within it, the dominant framework runs in the opposite direction. The mainstream belief in said discourse is as such: felt identity is primary while erotic experience is secondary, it's downstream. This is beyond a mere correction to Blanchardian typology as it is its own regulatory demand, a different way of organizing the same ambiguous experience into a legible narrative. It operates through the same mechanism the Blanchardian typology was operating on and it's enforced through the same citational mechanisms that produce the aesthetic cluster mentioned in the way that to narrate your experience the wrong way within the community, to suggest that in your experience the erotic dimension preceded or shaped the identity rather than following from it is to risk the kind of illegibility that the matrix reserves for its abject figures. Consider the person who encounters the community in a state of genuine ambiguity, who has not yet organized their experience into a coherent narrative. Who is not even certain if the erotic dimension preceded or followed their identity and yet at the same time finds the community's citational chain resonant without knowing why. They, in this context, are essentially being handed a hermeneutic, a way of reading and interpreting themselves. This means that the community's interpretive framework has arrived before the person developed their own and it's not presented as one way among several but as the only truth out there that is waiting to be recognized. Essentially an Althusserian interpellation that produces the subject it appears merely to address. The issue here isn't that the hermeneutic or the community's way of interpretation is wrong, it may be right for many of the people it reaches. The problem is that it cannot distinguish between the person whom it is organizing genuine pre-existing suffering, making them find the words for that suffering and the person for whom it is producing an experience that would not otherwise have achieved that particular form precisely for the reason that the individual in this context was presented a singular way of interpretation and marked it as the final truth.

The transsexual matrix reveals something that isn't unique to trans people specifically but something about how all subject positions are formed, that is through citation, repudiation, the slow accumulation of normative pressure until the mechanism disappears and only the identity remains. What is unusual isn't the process itself but the visibility and rapidity in which the process operates within the transsexual matrix. The citational chain is young enough (in contrast to identity-adjacent stereotypes) that some of the people who were present at its formation are still online and participating in the very process I've been describing. The transsexual matrix, like the heterosexual one is producing subjects in a Foucauldian sense and it should be aware of the kind of power that this holds.
